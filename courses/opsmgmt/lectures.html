Operations Management Fundamentals

What Is Operations Management?

Operations management (OM) is the administration of business practices to create the highest level of efficiency possible within an organization. It is concerned with converting materials and labor into goods and services as efficiently as possible to maximize profit — or, in public/social organizations, to maximize value delivery.

Operations management covers every activity involved in producing a product or delivering a service: design, planning, control, improvement, and strategy. It applies to both manufacturing (physical goods) and services (intangible outputs).

Efficiency vs. Effectiveness

  • Efficiency (doing things right): using the minimum resources (time, money, materials) to produce a given output. Measured as output ÷ input. A highly efficient operation wastes nothing.
  • Effectiveness (doing the right things): achieving the desired outcome or goal. An effective operation produces what customers actually want.
  • Good operations management pursues both: being efficient and effective simultaneously. A factory that produces 10,000 units cheaply but the wrong product is efficient but not effective.

Process Types

Operations can be classified by their process type, which determines how production is organized:

  • Project process: unique, one-off outputs, high customization (e.g., building a bridge, making a film). Long duration, high cost, complex coordination.
  • Jobbing (job shop) process: small batches of highly customized products (e.g., bespoke tailoring, custom machine parts). Flexible but slow.
  • Batch process: groups of similar products moved together through production stages (e.g., bakery, clothing manufacturer). Balances flexibility and efficiency.
  • Mass (line) process: high volume, low variety, standardized products (e.g., car assembly, consumer electronics). Highly efficient, low flexibility.
  • Continuous process: non-stop, automated production of a commodity (e.g., oil refining, electricity generation). Maximum efficiency, zero variety.

Capacity Planning

Capacity is the maximum output that a process can produce in a given time period. Capacity planning ensures that a business can meet demand without over- or under-investing in resources.

  • Design capacity: the theoretical maximum output under ideal conditions.
  • Effective capacity: realistic maximum output accounting for maintenance, breaks, quality issues, and changeovers.
  • Capacity utilization: actual output ÷ design capacity × 100%. Target: typically 80–90% (100% leaves no room for maintenance or demand spikes).
  • Bottleneck: the process step with the lowest capacity, which limits overall throughput. Managing bottlenecks is central to improving operations (Theory of Constraints, Goldratt).

Lean Production Systems

What Is Lean?

Lean synchronization — or simply "Lean" — is a philosophy, method, and set of tools focused on delivering exactly what the customer wants, in the exact quantity, exactly when and where needed, at the lowest possible cost. It aims for rapid, smooth flow through processes by eliminating waste — defined as any activity that does not add value.

Lean can be understood through three complementary perspectives:

  • Philosophy: founded on staff involvement, continuous improvement (Kaizen), and the relentless elimination of waste. Originally derived from the Toyota Production System (TPS), developed in Japan after World War II.
  • Method of Planning and Control: uses "pull" systems like Kanban to synchronize flow and reduce the unpredictability that generates waste.
  • Set of Tools: includes techniques such as Value-Stream Mapping (VSM) and SMED to improve operational performance.

James Womack and Daniel Jones popularized lean in the West through their 1990 book The Machine That Changed the World and later coined the term "lean thinking."

Lean vs. Traditional Approaches

Traditional operations use buffer inventories to insulate each stage from the one before and after it — absorbing disruptions and keeping each area running independently. Lean deliberately removes these buffers. Without buffers, problems are immediately exposed and cannot be hidden. This is intentional: exposed problems create urgency to solve them permanently rather than work around them.

The contrast in control logic is equally important:

  • Push control (traditional): each stage produces as much as possible and "pushes" output to the next stage, building up inventory in between.
  • Pull control (lean): each stage only produces what the next stage — ultimately the customer — actually requests. Nothing is produced without a signal of demand.

The Logic of Lean

Lean follows an internal logic that connects its principles:

  1. Customer focus defines what "value" means — only activities the customer values count.
  2. Customer focus drives pull — produce only in response to real demand.
  3. Pull encourages synchronized flow — processes align tightly, reducing queues and delays.
  4. Synchronized flow increases the motivation to expose and eliminate waste — because any disruption now halts the whole flow, problems must be solved, not buffered.

Waste: The DOWNTIME Framework

Waste (muda) is any activity that consumes resources without creating value. While Toyota's original model identified seven wastes (remembered as TIM WOOD), the modern lean framework extends this to eight types, captured by the acronym DOWNTIME:

  • D — Defects: products or services that fail to meet quality standards, requiring rework or scrapping.
  • O — Overproduction: producing more than the customer currently needs. Considered the worst waste — it generates all others.
  • W — Waiting: idle time when people, machines, or materials are waiting for the next process step.
  • N — Non-utilizing talent: failing to use the skills, knowledge, and creativity of employees. The waste of human potential.
  • T — Transportation: unnecessary movement of materials or products between locations.
  • I — Inventory: excess stock that is not being processed. Ties up capital, takes up space, and — critically — acts as a "blanket of obscurity" hiding underlying process problems.
  • M — Motion: unnecessary movement of people (walking, reaching, bending) in the workplace.
  • E — Extra-processing: doing more work or using more precision than the customer actually requires.

Kanban — Pull in Practice

Kanban (Japanese: "visual signal" or "card") is the primary tool for implementing pull control. A kanban card (or electronic equivalent) acts as an authorisation signal: a downstream stage sends a kanban upstream only when it needs more material or output. No kanban = no production. This prevents overproduction and limits work-in-progress to a defined maximum.

Key properties of a kanban system:

  • Production is triggered by actual consumption, not by a forecast.
  • The number of kanbans in circulation caps the amount of inventory in the system.
  • Reducing the number of kanbans deliberately exposes problems — a form of continuous improvement.

Kaizen — Continuous Improvement

Kaizen (Japanese: "change for better") is the practice of continuous, incremental improvement involving all employees — from the CEO to frontline workers. Rather than large, infrequent overhauls, kaizen focuses on making many small improvements every day.

  • Everyone is responsible for improvement — not just managers or engineers.
  • Improvements are based on data and observation, not assumptions.
  • Problems are seen as opportunities to improve, not failures to be hidden.
  • PDCA cycle (Plan-Do-Check-Act): the standard kaizen improvement cycle. Also known as the Deming Cycle.
  • Gemba Walk: managers physically visit the "actual place" (gemba) where work happens to observe processes firsthand, identify waste, and engage staff on improvement opportunities — rather than relying on reports.

Value-Stream Mapping (VSM)

Value-Stream Mapping is a lean tool used to visualize all steps — both value-adding and non-value-adding — in a product or service flow from raw material to the customer. It reveals where waste accumulates so improvement efforts can be focused. A VSM shows process steps, information flows, lead times, and inventory levels at each stage.

The four-step VSM process:

  1. Select a product family — choose the flow to map (a group of products that follow similar process steps).
  2. Draw the current-state map — document exactly how the process flows today, including all delays and inventory points.
  3. Draw the future-state map — redesign the flow to eliminate identified waste and move toward lean ideals.
  4. Create an implementation plan — define the actions, responsibilities, and timeline to close the gap between current and future states.

The 5S Method

5S is a workplace organisation methodology for creating and maintaining a clean, orderly, safe, and efficient environment. Each "S" is a Japanese term with an English equivalent:

  • Sort (Seiri): remove everything that is not needed from the work area.
  • Straighten (Seiton): organise and label what remains so everything has a designated place.
  • Shine (Seiso): clean the work area thoroughly and inspect as you clean.
  • Standardise (Seiketsu): establish standards and routines to maintain the first three S's consistently.
  • Sustain (Shitsuke): embed 5S as a habit through discipline, audits, and cultural reinforcement.

Key Terms

  • Kanban: a visual signal used to trigger movement or production based on actual downstream demand.
  • Kaizen: the Japanese concept of small, incremental, and continuous improvement involving all staff.
  • Gemba Walk: a practice of visiting the actual workplace to observe processes, identify waste, and find improvement opportunities firsthand.
  • DOWNTIME: acronym for the 8 types of waste — Defects, Overproduction, Waiting, Non-utilizing talent, Transportation, Inventory, Motion, Extra-processing.
  • VSM (Value-Stream Mapping): a lean tool to map current and future process states in order to identify and eliminate waste.
  • 5S: a housekeeping methodology — Sort, Straighten, Shine, Standardise, Sustain — for organising work areas.
  • Pull system: a control approach in which production is authorised only by actual downstream demand.
  • Push system: a control approach in which production is scheduled in advance and output is "pushed" to the next stage regardless of current need.

Critical Thinking Questions

  • Why does Lean describe inventory as a "blanket of obscurity"? What does removing inventory reveal, and why is that desirable?
  • How does "pull" control differ from "push" control in terms of waste generation? Which conditions favour each approach?
  • What are the potential drawbacks of Lean in a supply network subject to unexpected disturbances — for example, a pandemic or a natural disaster?

Quality Management

What Is Quality?

In operations management, quality means consistently meeting or exceeding customer expectations. Quality can be defined from multiple perspectives: the producer's (conformance to specifications), the customer's (fitness for purpose), and the market's (value for price paid).

Poor quality has cascading costs: rework, scrap, warranty claims, customer loss, brand damage, and regulatory penalties. Prevention costs are almost always lower than failure costs.

Total Quality Management (TQM)

TQM is a management approach that involves all employees in continuously improving the quality of products, services, and internal processes. Key principles:

  • Customer focus: quality is defined by what the customer values.
  • Employee involvement: quality is everyone's responsibility, not just the quality department.
  • Process-centered: understanding and improving processes is the key to quality improvement.
  • Continuous improvement: quality is never "finished" — there is always room to improve (linked to kaizen).
  • Data-driven decisions: improvements are based on measurement and analysis, not intuition.

Six Sigma

Six Sigma is a data-driven methodology for eliminating defects (aiming for fewer than 3.4 defects per million opportunities). Developed at Motorola and popularized by GE under Jack Welch in the 1990s.

Six Sigma uses the DMAIC cycle for improvement projects:

  • Define: define the problem, the goal, and the customer requirements.
  • Measure: measure the current process performance (baseline metrics).
  • Analyze: analyze data to identify the root causes of defects and variation.
  • Improve: design and implement solutions to address root causes.
  • Control: implement controls to sustain improvements and prevent regression.

Six Sigma practitioners are certified at different levels: Yellow Belt, Green Belt, Black Belt, and Master Black Belt.

ISO 9001 — Quality Management Systems

ISO 9001 is the international standard for Quality Management Systems (QMS). It provides a framework of requirements that organizations must meet to demonstrate their ability to consistently provide products and services that meet customer and regulatory requirements.

  • Based on seven quality management principles: customer focus, leadership, engagement of people, process approach, improvement, evidence-based decision making, relationship management.
  • Certification is awarded by accredited third-party bodies after an audit.
  • ISO 9001 certification is often required by customers and contracts, especially in B2B settings.

Operations Strategy & Performance

Operations Strategy

Operations strategy defines how an organization uses its operational resources and capabilities to support its business strategy. It answers: what should operations prioritize to help the business compete?

Operations strategy links corporate goals to operational decisions about capacity, technology, workforce, quality, and supplier relationships.

Competitive Priorities

Operations compete on four dimensions. An organization must choose which to prioritize (it is difficult to excel at all simultaneously):

  • Cost: producing goods or services at the lowest possible cost to offer competitive prices. Achieved through economies of scale, process automation, and lean practices.
  • Quality: producing goods or services that consistently meet or exceed customer expectations. High quality reduces defects and builds brand reputation.
  • Delivery speed & reliability: getting products to customers quickly and on time. Critical in industries with short lead times (fast fashion, food, e-commerce).
  • Flexibility: the ability to change quickly — offering a wide range of products, or responding rapidly to demand changes or custom orders.

Key Operations Performance Metrics

  • Overall Equipment Effectiveness (OEE): measures machine performance as Availability × Performance × Quality. World-class OEE ≈ 85%.
  • Throughput: units produced (or customers served) per unit of time. Maximizing throughput at the bottleneck improves overall output.
  • Cycle time: time required to complete one unit of production from start to finish.
  • Defect rate / First pass yield: percentage of units produced without defects on the first attempt.
  • On-time delivery (OTD): percentage of orders fulfilled by the promised delivery date.
  • Inventory turnover: how many times inventory is used and replenished in a period. Higher is generally better.

Sustainable Operations

Modern operations management increasingly integrates sustainability into its core strategy. This includes: reducing energy consumption and greenhouse gas emissions, designing for recyclability and circular flows, minimizing water use in manufacturing, and ensuring ethical treatment of workers across the supply chain. Sustainable operations reduce costs (energy, waste), manage regulatory risk, and meet growing customer and investor expectations.

L7: Supply Chain Management (SCM) — Supply Chain vs. Network & Sourcing

Abstract

Supply Chain Management (SCM) shifts the focus from internal operations to the holistic management of relationships and flows between a string of operations. This lecture focuses on how organizations manage the supply side through strategic sourcing, global configuration, and value creation across interconnected partners.

Defining the Supply Chain vs. Network

A supply network includes all operations linked to delivering products and services to end customers, while a supply chain is a specific strand of linked operations inside that broader network.

SCM is therefore the management of these relationships and flows to produce value for the final customer, not only the optimization of one internal function.

Sourcing Strategies and Risks

Managers must decide what to make internally and what to buy externally, and then design an appropriate supply base. Two key dimensions guide supplier strategy:

  • Supply risk: the number of alternative suppliers and the ease or difficulty of switching.
  • Criticality: how important the supplied item is for business growth and the share of total purchasing cost.

These choices shape whether firms rely on transactional relationships or build long-term partnerships with suppliers.

L8: Supply Chain Management (SCM) — Bullwhip Effect & Academic Support

Dynamics: The Bullwhip Effect

The bullwhip effect describes how small demand changes at the consumer end amplify into larger disturbances as information moves upstream toward suppliers.

Mitigating this effect requires better data visibility and coordination, including e-enabled networks, channel alignment, and operational efficiency across partners.

Glossary of Terms

  • SCM: management of relationships and flows between operations to produce value.
  • Global Sourcing: identifying and configuring supply across multiple geographies.
  • Bullwhip Effect: amplification of demand fluctuations as they move up the supply chain.

Project Management

What Is Project Management?

Project management is the activity of defining, planning, controlling, and learning from projects of any type. It involves balancing the "Iron Triangle" of Quality, Time, and Cost — any change to one constraint affects the other two.

Definition of a Project

A project is a set of activities with a defined start and end point, pursuing a specific goal using a defined set of resources. This distinguishes it from ongoing operations.

Projects vs. Programmes

Unlike a project, a programme (such as a continuous improvement initiative) has no defined end point — it is an ongoing process of change that continues indefinitely. Understanding this distinction matters when scoping and resourcing work.

The Project Environment

The project environment comprises all internal and external factors that may affect the project. Managing this environment involves:

  • Identifying stakeholders — all individuals or groups who have an interest in the project outcome.
  • Mapping stakeholders on a Power–Interest Grid to determine the appropriate level of engagement (e.g., "Manage Closely" vs. "Keep Informed").

The Planning Process

Planning is an iterative process that must be revisited as conditions change. It involves four key steps:

  • Work Breakdown Structure (WBS): breaking the project into manageable "work packages." The WBS acts like a "family tree" of tasks that shows how the project jigsaw fits together.
  • Estimating: using probabilistic estimates to identify the time and resource needs for each work package.
  • Dependencies: understanding which tasks must be done in series (sequential) and which can be done in parallel, in order to shorten overall duration.
  • Scheduling: using tools like Gantt charts for visual impact and communication. Gantt charts show task duration, dependencies, and float at a glance.

Key Terms to Know

  • Iron Triangle: the trade-off between cost, time, and quality — all three cannot be optimized simultaneously.
  • Stakeholders: individuals or groups who have an interest in the project outcome.
  • Work Breakdown Structure (WBS): the hierarchical decomposition of the project into manageable work packages.
  • Milestones vs. Stagegates: milestones mark the achievement or completeness of a task or phase; stagegates are formal decision points required to authorize progression to the next phase.

Critical Concepts for Analysis

  • Volume and Variety: all projects are generally low volume and high variety, but they still differ. "First-timer" projects have higher uniqueness than "paint-by-numbers" projects, demanding more planning effort.
  • Project Definition — the Three Elements: a project is fully defined by its Objectives (purpose/results), Scope (responsibilities/boundaries), and Strategy (the general approach).
  • Resource Levelling: adjusting a schedule when resources (such as staff) are overloaded — redistributing work across time to avoid bottlenecks without extending the project deadline where possible.
  • Float: the amount of time a task can be delayed without affecting the overall project end date. Illustrated by the "Breakfast-in-Bed" case — toast preparation can be deliberately delayed to ensure it arrives hot, without delaying the whole breakfast.

Practical Application: Stakeholder Mapping

Place project participants on a Power–Interest Grid:

  • High Power / High Interest → Manage Closely: engage fully and communicate frequently.
  • High Power / Low Interest → Keep Satisfied: meet their needs without overwhelming them.
  • Low Power / High Interest → Keep Informed: provide regular updates; they are advocates.
  • Low Power / Low Interest → Monitor: minimal effort; watch for changes in their position.

Self-Assessment Questions

  • How do scale, complexity, and uncertainty affect the difficulty of managing a project?
  • What are the five performance objectives used to judge project success?
  • Why is "project scoping" considered one of the most critical reasons projects fail?
  • In a Gantt chart, what does "float" tell you about a task, and how can it be exploited to improve quality?
