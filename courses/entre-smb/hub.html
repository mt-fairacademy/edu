01

What Is Entrepreneurship?

Defining Entrepreneurship

Entrepreneurship is the process of designing, launching, and running a new business venture, typically in response to an identified opportunity, while taking on financial and personal risk in pursuit of profit, growth, or social impact.

Modern definitions emphasize value creation: entrepreneurs create value for customers, employees, and society — not merely profit for themselves.

Types of Entrepreneur

  • Lifestyle entrepreneur: builds a business to support a desired lifestyle rather than to scale rapidly.
  • Serial entrepreneur: starts multiple businesses over time, often selling one venture before beginning another.
  • Social entrepreneur: applies entrepreneurial thinking to address social or environmental problems.
  • Intrapreneur: acts as an entrepreneur within an established organization — driving innovation from the inside.
  • Tech/Startup entrepreneur: builds a scalable technology-driven business aimed at rapid growth.

Small and Medium-Sized Enterprises (SMEs)

The EU defines SMEs by three criteria:

  • Micro: fewer than 10 employees; turnover or balance sheet ≤ €2 million.
  • Small: fewer than 50 employees; turnover or balance sheet ≤ €10 million.
  • Medium: fewer than 250 employees; turnover ≤ €50 million OR balance sheet ≤ €43 million.

SMEs represent 99% of all businesses in the EU, employ about two-thirds of the private sector workforce, and generate over half of the EU's GDP.

Innovation and Entrepreneurship

Joseph Schumpeter described entrepreneurship as a force of “creative destruction.” He identified four types of innovation:

  • Product innovation: new or significantly improved goods or services.
  • Process innovation: new or improved methods of production or delivery.
  • Market innovation: entering new markets or repositioning existing products.
  • Organizational innovation: new business models or management practices.

02

The Startup Mindset

What Is a Startup?

A startup is a young company founded to develop a unique product or service, bring it to market, and scale it rapidly. Steve Blank defines a startup as “a temporary organization designed to search for a repeatable and scalable business model.”

The Lean Startup Methodology

Eric Ries popularized the Lean Startup in 2011. Its core principle is to reduce waste by shortening product development cycles through rapid experimentation via the Build-Measure-Learn feedback loop:

  • Build: create the simplest possible version of your product (MVP) to test your assumptions.
  • Measure: collect data on how real customers respond to the MVP.
  • Learn: use the data to validate or invalidate your assumptions, then decide whether to persevere or pivot.

Minimum Viable Product (MVP)

An MVP is the simplest version of a product that can be released to early customers to gather validated learning — not a prototype but a real product.

  • Examples: Airbnb's first MVP was a simple website renting out the founders' apartment; Dropbox launched a demo video before building the product.
  • Key principle: the MVP must deliver the core value proposition — nothing more, nothing less.

Pivot

A pivot is a structured course correction while maintaining some elements of the original plan. Types include: customer segment pivot, value capture pivot, channel pivot, and product pivot.

Market Sizing: TAM, SAM, SOM

  • TAM: total global demand for your type of product or service.
  • SAM: the portion of TAM you can realistically reach with your current model.
  • SOM: your beachhead — the specific slice you can realistically capture in 1–3 years.

Build estimates Bottom-Up from specific segments — far more credible than carving a percentage off a giant total market figure.

Understanding the Customer's Problem

Problem-Solution Fit

Before building anything, entrepreneurs must confirm that (a) a real, significant problem exists, (b) the target customers are motivated to solve it, and (c) the proposed solution genuinely addresses it. Many startups fail because they build solutions looking for problems.

Customer Discovery

Customer discovery (Steve Blank) is the process of talking to potential customers to understand their problems before building products.

  • Interviews: open-ended conversations focused on customer pain points and desired outcomes.
  • Observation: watch customers in their natural environment to understand actual behavior.
  • The Mom Test (Rob Fitzpatrick): ask about people's lives, not your idea. Past behavior is more reliable than future intention.

Behavioral vs. hypothetical questions:

  • Bad: “Would you use an app that tracks your waste?”
  • Good: “Tell me about the last time you had to throw away unsold stock. What did you do?”

Jobs-to-be-Done Theory

Clayton Christensen: customers don't buy products — they “hire” them to do a specific job. Types: functional (practical tasks), social (how they want to be perceived), emotional (how they want to feel).

The JTBD example: people who buy a drill don't want a drill — they want a hole in the wall. Understanding the deeper job reveals real innovation opportunities.

Pain Points

  • Financial pain: solutions that cost too much, or processes that waste money.
  • Productivity pain: tasks that take too long or are too complex.
  • Process pain: inefficient workflows or missing steps.
  • Support pain: inadequate help or information when customers need it.

Problem Definition & Ideation

Design Thinking

Design thinking is a human-centered approach to innovation popularized by IDEO and the Stanford d.school. A proper problem description focuses on the beneficiary's current reality — it does not jump to a solution.

Problem statement structure: Who is affected · What undesirable situation they face · Impact (human or economic consequence).

Example: “Small artisanal bakers in Florence (Who) are losing 30% of their daily inventory due to unpredictable tourist foot traffic (What), leading to financial instability and food waste (Impact).”

The five stages: Empathize → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test (iterative — return to earlier stages as needed).

How Might We…?

The HMW format frames problems broad enough to allow creative solutions but narrow enough to be actionable. Example: “How might we help commuters find parking quickly near their destination?”

Root Cause Analysis: The 5 Whys

Repeatedly ask “Why?” (typically five times) until the fundamental cause is uncovered. Developed within Toyota's production system.

Example: “Customers aren't buying our organic soap.” → They think it's too expensive → They compare to mass-market brands → They don't see the value → The packaging looks the same as cheap soap. Root cause: visual branding fails to communicate the premium value proposition.

Value Proposition

A clear statement of the unique value your product creates for a specific customer segment. The Value Proposition Canvas (Osterwalder) maps customer jobs, pains, and gains against pain relievers and gain creators your offering provides.

Marketing for Startups & SMEs

The Marketing Mix — 4Ps

  • Product: features, quality, design, branding, packaging.
  • Price: cost-plus, value-based, penetration, or skimming strategies.
  • Place (Distribution): DTC, retail, e-commerce, or marketplace platforms.
  • Promotion: advertising, PR, social media, content marketing, email.

Modern extensions include the 7Ps (adding People, Process, Physical Evidence) — particularly relevant for service businesses.

Traditional vs. Entrepreneurial Marketing — The 4Is Model

  • Information: informal networking and data gathering through relationships.
  • Innovation: product or service quality as the primary marketing tool.
  • Identification: finding specific niches where the SME can be the best option.
  • Interaction: building direct social relationships — turning buyers into advocates.

Digital Marketing for Startups

  • SEO: optimizing content to rank higher in organic search results.
  • Content Marketing: creating valuable content to attract, engage, and convert customers.
  • Social Media: building community and brand awareness on key platforms.
  • Email Marketing: high-ROI channel (average: $36 per $1 spent).
  • Paid Advertising (PPC/SEM): rapid, targeted reach via Google Ads and Meta Ads.

Key Marketing Metrics: CAC and LTV

  • CAC: total marketing & sales spend ÷ new customers acquired.
  • LTV: average purchase value × purchase frequency × customer lifespan.
  • LTV:CAC ratio: healthy startups aim for LTV ≥ 3× CAC.
  • Churn Rate: % of customers who stop using your product in a given period.

Future Marketing Trends (2026)

  • AI Fluency: use AI for effectiveness (better decisions, targeting, personalization) — not just efficiency.
  • AI Transparency: consumers demand honesty about AI-generated content.
  • Influencer Regulation: formal advertising rules now apply — sponsored content must be clearly disclosed.

Business Structures — Startups & Franchises

Startup vs. Franchise

  • Startup: built from scratch — complete autonomy but high risk and full burden of brand-building.
  • Franchise: the franchisee pays the franchisor for the right to operate under an established brand and proven model. Trading autonomy for security.

Franchising — Benefits and Drawbacks

Benefits: management training, instant brand recognition, standardized quality, site selection assistance.

Drawbacks: ongoing royalties (1–11% of gross sales), strict adherence to standardized operations, restricted product lines, brand reputation outside your control.

Società Benefit (Italy)

An Italian legal form that pursues profit alongside an explicit “common benefit” — social or environmental goals beyond shareholder return.

  • Pros: market differentiation (especially in Luxury & Fashion), higher employee retention, legal protection of the founder's mission.
  • Cons: must publish an annual Impact Report and appoint an Impact Manager.

Why Businesses Fail

  • No market need: the most common cause — building a solution to a problem that isn't painful enough.
  • Running out of cash: even profitable businesses fail if cash flow timing is mismanaged.
  • The Competency Trap: businesses keep doing what they're good at, even when the market changes.

07

Business Planning

Business Model Canvas vs. Business Plan

  • BMC — “The Sketch”: a one-page visual chart mapping value proposition, infrastructure, customers, and finances. Used to iterate and pivot quickly during validation.
  • Business Plan — “The Blueprint”: a 20–40 page document serving as internal roadmap and external document to attract capital.

Two Primary Functions of a Business Plan

  • Internal: guides strategy, defines roles and milestones, keeps the team aligned.
  • External: convinces lenders and investors that the business is viable and financials are realistic.

The 5 Cs of Credit

  • Character: your reputation and track record.
  • Capacity: can the business generate enough cash flow to repay the loan? Most heavily weighted.
  • Capital: how much of your own money (“skin in the game”) are you investing?
  • Collateral: what assets can be pledged to secure the loan?
  • Conditions: external climate — inflation, industry trends, loan purpose.

Pitching the Plan

  • Hook early: lead with the customer problem and the scale of opportunity — not product features.
  • Show enthusiasm: investors back people as much as ideas.
  • Be realistic: overly optimistic forecasts destroy credibility.
  • Avoid jargon overload: use visual aids and plain language.

Glossary of Key Terms
TermDefinition
Lean StartupA methodology focusing on shortened product development cycles and validated learning through the Build-Measure-Learn loop.
PivotA structured course correction to test a new hypothesis about the product, customer segment, or strategy — based on data, not failure.
TAM / SAM / SOMTotal Addressable Market / Serviceable Addressable Market / Serviceable Obtainable Market. The three levels of market sizing.
4Is ModelInformation, Innovation, Identification, and Interaction — the four pillars of entrepreneurial (SME) marketing.
Società BenefitItalian legal form for companies that pursue profit and a specific public or environmental benefit, requiring an annual impact report.
5 Cs of CreditThe criteria banks use to evaluate business loan applications: Character, Capacity, Capital, Collateral, and Conditions.
Market IntelligenceThe sum of formal data and informal networking gathered to understand demand, trends, and competitors.
Competency TrapThe tendency of a business to keep doing what it is good at, even when the market changes — preventing necessary adaptation.
Iterative InterviewingInterviewing customers repeatedly, in rounds, to progressively refine understanding of their pains and the root cause of the problem.
Business Model CanvasA one-page visual tool mapping a business's value proposition, infrastructure, customer relationships, and revenue streams.
